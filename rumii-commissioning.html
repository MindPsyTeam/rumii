The Invisible Gap

Healthcare operates blind between contacts

Most healthcare systems are episodic: a person is seen, assessed, and discharged to a follow-up weeks or months away. In between, there is no visibility. No signal. No way to know whether someone is stable, improving, or quietly falling apart.

This gap is most dangerous for populations where help-seeking is delayed, stigma is high, and behavioural deterioration is gradual — young veterans transitioning out of service, emerging adults managing long-term conditions, young people falling between CAMHS and adult services. These are people in the most mobile and unstable period of their lives, often relocating, rarely registered with a consistent GP, and unlikely to initiate contact until things are already bad. By the time they present again, the window for early intervention has closed.

Jamie's story is a veteran's story — but the pattern is universal across emerging adults. A 22-year-old with Crohn's disease whose flare builds silently between clinic visits. A young person discharged from CAMHS who moves to university and falls off every service radar simultaneously. A care leaver navigating independent living with no structured support. The structural problem is the same: episodic care creates a blind spot, and young people — who are mobile, reluctant to seek help, and rarely in one system long enough to be tracked — fall through it.

The Rumii Approach

Passive sensing. Adaptive support. Intelligent escalation.

Rumii is a Just-in-Time Adaptive Intervention (JITAI) — its monitoring layer runs quietly on the person's smartphone, requiring no check-ins, no questionnaires, no daily logging. It learns each individual's unique baseline and detects drift from that baseline. When it responds, it does so through adaptive conversations that meet the person where they are.

1

Learn the individual baseline

Rumii uses passive smartphone sensors — accelerometer, GPS patterns, device interaction behaviour, and connectivity — to build a behavioural fingerprint unique to each person. No content is accessed. No messages read. No apps monitored. The sensing layer is entirely privacy-preserving: it measures how someone interacts with their device physically, not what they do on it. The phone they already carry becomes the clinical instrument.

Passive · Privacy-preserving
2

Detect drift and support improvement

Rumii tracks changes in sleep rhythm, social contact, routine regularity, and daily structure against the individual's own baseline — not a population average. This means the system recognises when your behaviour shifts, rather than comparing you to everyone else. But personalised baselines are only half the picture: Rumii also supports active improvement in behavioural health, helping people build toward healthier patterns over time — not just monitoring whether they stay where they started.

Individual-baseline Improvement-oriented
3

Deliver adaptive behavioural activation

When drift is detected, Rumii's reasoning agent selects the right intervention at the right moment — compassion-focused behavioural prompts delivered conversationally. The system adapts based on the person's current behavioural state, not a fixed protocol. If someone is withdrawing socially, the response differs from when their sleep has fragmented or their routine has collapsed.

Adaptive · Clinically grounded
4

Surface risk and signpost appropriately

When Rumii is integrated into a care pathway, it surfaces phenotype changes and deterioration trajectories to the clinical team — enabling clinicians to decide whether to re-engage with the person sooner than scheduled. Rumii provides the signal; the clinical judgement remains with the clinician. Where there is no pathway integration, the system signposts the person directly — to crisis services where the need is acute, or to local community care and support where the need is less immediate.

The critical distinction: Rumii measures change from the individual's established behavioural baseline, not deviation from a population norm. This makes it fundamentally more inclusive — a veteran with a pre-existing physical disability or someone with an irregular daily routine is assessed against their own normal, not someone else's. Where behaviours fall outside healthy thresholds — such as disrupted circadian rhythms — the system supports improvement, not just monitoring.

The Reasoning Agent keeps all conversational support clinically grounded and evidence-based, while adjusting the tone and timing of that support to the individual's readiness for change and preferred interaction style. It integrates real-time behavioural signals with longitudinal patterns to determine when to engage and how — operating within version-locked clinical protocols throughout.

Evidence Base

Built on established research, not speculation

Rumii's approach draws on three converging bodies of peer-reviewed evidence. The principles underlying the platform — adaptive intervention, passive sensing, and early intervention economics — are well-established in the literature.

1

Just-in-Time Adaptive Interventions improve mental health outcomes

A 2025 meta-analysis of 23 RCTs (N=2,563) found JITAIs produce statistically significant improvements in depression and anxiety, with effects sustained at 3–6 month follow-up. Shorter, targeted intervention periods yielded the strongest lasting effects — supporting an approach that intervenes early and briefly rather than running fixed-length programmes.

Beishuizen et al. (2025) BMJ Mental Health · Nahum-Shani et al. (2018) Annals of Behavioral Medicine

2

Passive smartphone sensing detects deterioration before crisis

Systematic reviews consistently demonstrate that passive sensor data — movement, sleep, social contact, device interaction — can predict mental health deterioration weeks before clinical presentation. Studies in schizophrenia found behavioural anomalies increase by over 100% in the 30 days before relapse, detectable through smartphone data alone.

Adler et al. (2020) Schizophrenia Research · Cornet & Holden (2018) J. Biomedical Informatics · Seppälä et al. (2019) JMIR mHealth

3

Early intervention reduces NHS costs substantially

A longitudinal study across the Oxford Academic Health Science Network found early intervention services saved a mean of £4,031 per patient annually in NHS costs, primarily through reduced inpatient admissions. Extrapolated nationally, the estimated saving would be £33.5 million per year in NHS costs alone. Separately, real-world analysis of 27,540 NHS patients confirmed that waiting time and treatment effectiveness are the principal drivers of healthcare cost — exactly the variables that continuous monitoring and adaptive support compress.

Tsiachristas et al. (2016) BMJ Open · Catarino et al. (2023) Nature Mental Health

↳ Primary Use Case — Veterans

Continuous monitoring for young veterans between healthcare contacts

Early service leavers — those leaving the military in their late teens and twenties — are the highest-risk cohort for the gap problem. They leave a highly structured environment, often without the transition support available to longer-serving personnel, and enter civilian life with high self-reliance, delayed help-seeking patterns, and a learned capacity to suppress distress signals. They relocate frequently, register with new GPs who have no history, and rarely identify as "someone who needs support."

Rumii provides the continuous behavioural visibility that episodic care cannot — detecting the gradual withdrawal, sleep disruption, and routine collapse that precede crisis, and responding before the veteran reaches A&E. It travels with them regardless of where they move.

✓ Individual-baseline sensing — inclusive of veterans with disabilities, irregular routines, or existing conditions
✓ Compassion-focused activation — clinically grounded conversational support calibrated to the veteran's behavioural state

Emerging adults living with IBD, diabetes, or chronic pain

Young people managing chronic conditions face a double burden: the condition itself and the instability of early adult life — moving for university or work, changing GPs, managing treatment independently for the first time. Behavioural drift often precedes a clinical flare by weeks. Rumii detects the behavioural precursors and intervenes before the condition escalates, reducing unplanned secondary care contacts.

Post-Discharge

After acute mental health admission (18–30)

The weeks following discharge from acute mental health services are the highest-risk period for relapse and readmission. For emerging adults, discharge often coincides with return to unstable housing, fragmented social networks, and limited community support. Rumii bridges the gap with continuous monitoring and graduated support during the transition back to independent living.

Key signals: social withdrawal, temporal dysregulation, reduced engagement
Transition Gap

CAMHS to adult services

The transition gap between child and adult mental health services is where many young people disengage from care entirely — often coinciding with moving away for education or employment. Continuous behavioural monitoring through this transition maintains visibility during the most vulnerable period, with adaptive support and escalation pathways configured to local service structures.

Key signals: screen time shifts, social pattern changes, routine collapse

↳ The Case for Earlier Intervention

Catching deterioration earlier changes the economics of care

When behavioural drift is detected weeks before crisis, the downstream impact cascades across the system. Earlier intervention supports quicker recovery — a person reached at the point of emerging withdrawal responds to lower-intensity support than one presenting in acute crisis. Quicker recovery means better flow through the system: shorter episodes of care, fewer follow-ups, and reduced pressure on already stretched services.

The cost argument is structural, not marginal. Every crisis presentation avoided is an A&E attendance, a potential inpatient admission, a crisis team deployment, and a longer recovery trajectory that did not need to happen. Rumii shifts the point of contact from reactive crisis response to proactive early support — reducing higher-cost service utilisation and freeing clinical capacity for the people who need it most.
